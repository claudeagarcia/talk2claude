Ideas that restore agency

The Frameworks

I

The Five Archetypes

Why do some people act on crises while others deny, ignore, or simply can't engage? A typology of decision-makers — from the Uninformed to the Architect — that maps the barriers between knowing and acting.

Decision-making

The framework proposes five archetypes of decision-makers: the Uninformed, who lack access to information; the Denier, who rejects evidence; the Occupied, who is consumed by immediate survival; the Concerned, who cares but feels powerless; and the Architect, who has the information, belief, values, and means to act.

Each archetype is defined by four nested hypotheses about information, beliefs, values, and means. The solution space maps trade-offs between human well-being and nature, revealing why good intentions alone never suffice.

Published in Waeber et al. (2021), Choices We Make in Times of Crisis, Sustainability journal.

II

The Four Gates

Authoritarian regimes don't just oppose climate action — they systematically block it through four sequential barriers. A framework for understanding how power structures perform climate counterinsurgency.

Climate governance

The Four Gates theory identifies the sequential barriers that authoritarian systems erect to prevent environmental action: controlling information, shaping beliefs, manipulating values, and restricting means.

Each gate must be passed in sequence. Understanding this architecture reveals why scattered resistance fails and why strategic, gate-specific interventions are essential. The framework applies not only to authoritarian regimes but to any system where power concentrations block collective action.

Currently being developed for submission to high-impact venues including Science and Foreign Affairs.

III

Systems Typology

Six hierarchical levels from inert physical structures to normative governance systems. A unified framework bridging natural sciences, social sciences, and philosophy into a single coherent architecture.

Systems theory

The typology traces a progression from inert structures through adaptive systems, cognitive agents, social systems, institutional frameworks, to normative governance — each level emergent from and dependent on those below.

This framework connects consciousness emergence to representational capacity, offering a philosophical foundation for understanding why governance is not merely a political problem but a systems problem rooted in how complexity organizes itself.

IV

PowerShift & LEAF

Strategy games that put real policy dilemmas in people's hands. Used across continents to help communities and governments experience the consequences of their choices before making them.

Participatory modeling

LEAF Inspiring Change is a spinoff organization that develops experiential learning tools. PowerShift and related games immerse participants in real governance scenarios where every decision has cascading consequences.

Deployed in collaboration with AFD, BirdLife International, IUCN, and governments across Colombia, Democratic Republic of Congo, São Tomé, Senegal, and beyond. The games make abstract policy dilemmas visceral and personal — and that changes how people decide.

"I had no idea" is maybe the most powerful feedback anyone has ever said to me.
